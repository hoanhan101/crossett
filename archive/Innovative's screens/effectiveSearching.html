Selecting Search Terms

Before you begin researching your topic, spend some time brainstorming. What is your thesis statement or question that you want to answer? Write it down and a sheet of paper.

How did social philosophies of the day fuel
the industrial revolution in England and America?

Identify the key concepts of your topic. Then, consider what terms or phrases are synonymous with your key concepts. Write them down as well.

Industrial Revolution Social Philosophy America
18th Century 1900's Britian
England Industralization Late Eighteenth Century
Early Nineteenth Century 19th Century United States (of America)
AND probably lots more...

Begin utilizing your key concepts as search terms in information resources. You can search reference books, academic journals, and publications through the databases and our catalog. As you find materials that are relevant to your topic, evaluate them for new, more specific search terms that might assist your research.

How to Evaluate a Scholarly Resource

The Difference Between the Library Catalog, the Databases, and the World Wide Web | Evaluating Online Resources

There are many types of journals and magazines, ranging from serious academic journals to sensational tabloid publications. Academic research often requires that you use scholarly and professional journals as your sources. A scholarly journal provides articles of primary research in a specific field or discipline. The main purpose of a scholarly journal is to report on original research or experimentation in order to make such information available to the rest of the scholarly world. Many scholarly journals require that articles included for publication be subject to a {u201C}peer review{u201D} process, in which means other scholars in the field must evaluate articles submitted to the journal for their intellectual integrity.


If you are trying to determine whether your source material is appropriate, check with your teacher or with a librarian. In general, scholarly journals have a serious, professional appearance. They may contain graphs or charts but few glossy pages or pictures. Scholarly journals always cite their sources in the form of footnotes or bibliographies. The language of scholarly journals is that of the discipline covered. It assumes some scholarly background on the part of the reader.


Evaluating Web Resources

Jump to the The Difference Between the Library Catalog, the Databases, and the World Wide Web

One must think critically about the source of information on the web in order to assess its relevance. The date, author/hosts, intended audience, and intended purpose of the information can give valuable cues to gauge the reliability and authenticity of online resources.

Here are questions that one should ask his/herself while conducting research online.

Authority

  • Who created the sources content? What relevance or authority does the individual/group have in regards to the topic?
  • Domains {.com, .edu, .org}. Click here to learn more.
    • Domains can indicate the type of group (and potential motivations) that maintain the information in question.
  • (if a web site) What are, if any, supporting or affiliated sites?
    • Are specific companies, government organizations, NGOs, Non-profits, etc. supporting the site - financially or otherwise?

Accuracy

  • Is the factual information easily verifiable(properly cited)?
  • Does the author provide contact information along with his/her/ its content?

Objectivity

  • What is the thesis/hypothesis of the content of the source?
  • Is there a clear bias to the source?
  • Is the source/site comprehensive in its presentation of the topic?
    • Are the supplemental links comprehensive or are they a sampling of a particular bias?
  • In relation to other available resources (electronic and print) on your topic, is this source of value?
    Check with a librarian to ensure that you are considering all resource options for your topic.

Currency

  • Are there dates indicating whether the information is current?
  • Consider the different dates supplied against each other in order to assess the currency of the source.
    • Date created
    • Date updated
    • Date posted
    • Date revised
  • Is there indication that the page-site-source is {u201C}under construction{u201D}? If so, the completeness of the content could be in doubt.

Purpose/Audience

  • For whom is the source intended? Was this source written to inform, persuade, or entertain the target audience?
  • What was the purpose of creating the source?

Please feel free to consult a reference librarian regarding assistance with evaluating web resources.

The difference between the Library Catalog, the Databases, and the World Wide Web

The library{u2019}s catalog allows you to find books and other materials that are physically located in Crossett Library. It also allows you to find ebooks owned by the library. You can access the catalog from anywhere through the web.

The databases are fee-based subscription services that are not available to anyone using the web, but only to subscribing institutions like Bennington College. You can access these databases from anywhere on campus through the web.

The web refers to the network of individual sites that are available to anyone. Using web sites for research requires you to be especially critical of each site's source and contents.

How to Read Call Numbers

Libraries use call numbers to group books together by subject. When a library uses call numbers to organize its books, researchers can browse the library's collection by subject areas such as psychology, history or music. Call numbers provide a unique "address" for every item within a library, so that once you understand the structure of call numbers, you can locate any item you are seeking. Because call numbers group items by subject, once you find one relevant item, you can browse the shelves around it to find other items on the same or similar subjects.

When you use our online catalog, your search results will give you lists of books and their respective call numbers. Crossett Library, like many academic libraries, uses Library of Congress classification for its call numbers.

Example: LB 2395 .C65 1991

Read the first line in alphabetical order:

A, B, BF, C, D... L, LA,LB, LC, M, ML...

Read the second line as a whole number:

1, 2, 3, 45, 100, 101, 1000, 2000, 2395, 2430...

The third line is a combination of a letter and numbers. Read the letter alphabetically. Read the number as a decimal, with each numeral considered separately.

.C65 = .65 .C724 = .724

For example, .N420 would be filed ahead of .N72. First the letter would be read, then the first digit--4 before 7.

    Some call numbers have more than one combination letter-number line.

The last line is the year the book was published. Read in chronological order:

1985, 1991, 1992...
